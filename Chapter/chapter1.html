Chapter 1 The introduction to Quantum Computing

1.1 What is Quantum computer?

Quantum computer is a machine constructed based on the principles of quantum mechanics to perform computations. Richard Feynman fisrt proposed the idea that a quantum computer, based on the principles of quantum mechanics, could effectively simulate the behaviour of quantum systems. The original motivation behind Feynman's proposal arised from the inherent challenges classical computers faced in modeling quantum systems. The limitations of classical computers became increasingly evident as researchers sought to simulate complex quantum phenomena. Feynman suggested the concept of quantum computer, designed to utilize the unique properties of quantum mechanics to perform simulations more effectively. His proposal laid the groundwork for the field of quantum computing. Various types of quantum computers have been built and researchers are exporing the development of quantum computers, designing quantum algorithms for solving certain problems faster than classical one. The pursuit of practical quantum computing is fueled by the potential to fields such as optimization, cryptography, etc.

1.2 Types of quantum computers

Quantum computers are caregorized into different types based on the physical qubits. Common types include superconducting quantum computers, trapped-ion quantum computers, semiconductor quantum computers topoligical quantum computers, quantum annealers, and photonic quantum computers. Each approach has its unique set of advantages and challenges and is discussed as follows.

1.2.1 Superconducting quantum computers

Superconducting quantum computers rely on superconducting circuits to manipulate quantum bits. It utilizes superconducting materials, which has zero resistance around absolute zero temperature. Quantum bits can be prepared by Josephson Junctions. The Josephson Junction is a device in superconductivity that consists of two superconductors separated by a thin insulating or non-superconducting barrier. The key feature of the Josephson Junction is to allow supercurrent to flow across the barrier. The quantum phenomenon can be observed in Josephson junctions.

There are three types of superconducting quantum qubits, charge qubit, flux qubit, and phase qubit, where U represents a voltage, Cg represents a capacitance, L represents an inductance, \(I_{0}\) represents a current, and \(E_{J}\) represents a Josephson junction.

Transmon qubit is the most common one of the charge qubit. It is a variation of the original Cooper pair box qubit, improving coherence and properties and easier control. One key feature is the energy difference between the qubit's ground and excited states is much larger than the thermal energy at the operating temperature. In addition, it has less noise and long coherence times.

Advantages

▪High Designability: superconducting qubits, including charge qubits, flux qubits, and phase qubits, offer a high level of design flexibility.

▪Scalability: superconducting qubits are prepared using semiconductor microfabrication processes, allowing for the leveraging of advanced chip-making technologies and the production of high-quality devices.

▪Easy to couple: superconducting qubits can be efficiently coupled using either capacitance or inductance, providing ease in creating interactions between qubits.

▪Easy to control: microwave control is compatible with the measurement of superconducting qubits, simplifying the control measurements.

Challenges

▪Short coherence time: decoherence time in superconducting qubits can be limited, impacting stability and reliability of quantum information processing.

▪Qubits not true 2-level systems: other unrelated quantum states exist, introducing complexities in their interactions.

▪Low temperature and advanced cryostats: Operating at sufficient low temperature, often close to absolute zero, is a requirement for superconducting qubits. The use of advanced cryostats maintains the condition of quantum coherence.
